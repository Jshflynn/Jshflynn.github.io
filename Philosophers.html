Thales
(c. 624/623 – c. 548/545 BC)
Everything is water in one form or another.

Anaximander
(610 - 546 BC)
The earth is a cylindrical solid object hanging in space, equidistant to everything else.

Anaximenes
(585 - 528 BC)
The Earth is flat and floats on air.

Pythagoras
(570 - 497 BC)
We remember a lot of what we know from a previous life.
Mathematical order pervades the physical world.

Xenophanes
(570 - 475 BC)
We may get nearer and nearer to the truth but our knowledge is our creation; it is in fact conjecture.
Each group shapes their gods in the likeness of their own.

Heraclitus
(535 - 475 BC)
Everything is a coming together of opposites.
There are no stable 'things'. Everything is flux - changing all the time.
A hidden connection is stronger than an obvious one.
It is not possible to step into the same river twice.

Parmenides
(515 - 460 BC)
There can never have been nothing; everything must always have existed.
It is not possible for anything to pass into nothing; everything is eternal.
The universe is a single unchanging entity; all is one.
What appears as change is something that occurs within an enclosed and unchanging system.

Empedocles
(490 - 430 BC)
Everything is made up of four elements that are perennial: earth, water, air, and fire.

Leucippus and Democritus
(5th century BC)
Everything is made up of indivisible particles (atoms) that are too small to be seen.
All change in the universe consists of atoms altering formations or locations.
The universe is not a continuum, but consists of separate entities.

Zeno of Elea
(490 - 430 BC)
An impeccably logical argument can lead to a false conclusion.

Socrates
(470 - 399 BC)
Knowledge of the universe does not affect our behaviour.
We need to know how to conduct our lives and ourselves.
Things like 'justice' do exist; not materially, but in some sort of essence.
To a man who preserves his integrity no real, long-term harm can ever come.
We should pity the perpetrator of injustice, not the victim of injustice.
No one really knowingly does wrong.
There can be no cut and dried answers; answers themselves are open to question.

Plato
(427 - 347 BC)
Virtue is not solely a matter of knowing what is right.
The only harm that can come to a person is harm to the soul.
It is better to suffer wrong than to commit it.
Everything and everybody should be questioned, taking nothing for granted.
Everything is an ephemeral copy of a permanent idea (or Form) outside of space and time.
Underlying our messy world, there is another reality with all the perfection of mathematics.
The underlying perfect reality is accessible to the mind and the intellect.
We are born possessing all knowledge; when an Idea is 'learned' it is actually just 'recalled'.
In our visible world with space and time, everything is becoming, nothing is.
Our bodies are ephemeral; our souls are our permanent Forms, timeless and indestructible.
Our aim in life should be to penetrate to the level of underlying reality.
Art is doubly deceptive, creating illusory semblances of illusory semblances.
Man is made up of three conflicting elements: passion, intellect, and will.
Intellect should govern passions through the will.
A philosophically aware class should govern society through an intermediate police class.
Women share by nature in every way of life just as men do, but in all of them women are weaker than men.
Girls should be brought up on equal terms with boys.

Aristotle
(384 - 322 BC)
There is only one world what we can philosophize about - the one we live in.
If we stray beyond the ground covered by experience we wander into empty talk.
A thing is whatever it is by virtue of its form.
The material cause of a thing is what it is made of.
The efficient cause of a thing is what does or makes it.
The formal cause of a thing is the shape by which it's identified.
The final cause of a thing is the ultimate reason for it.
An object's form, though not material, is inherent in this world.
The real point of everything is what it does, what it is for.
Change occurs when the ongoing material that is part of something acquires a new form.
What we want is a happy life.
To be happy, we must be compatible with living in a society; personal isolation is no good.
A virtue is the midway point between two extremes; our aim should be balance.
The purpose of government is to enable its citizens to live the happy life.
Poetry is more philosophical and more worthy of attention than history.
When we watch a tragedy, we experience catharsis - cleansing by pity and terror.
All men by nature desire to know.
The male is by nature superior and the female inferior, the male ruler and the female subject.

Diogenes
(404 - 323 BC)
The only thing that matters is the difference between true values and false values.
All social conventions (public/private, naked/clothed, etc.) are nonsense.
I am a citizen of the world.

Pyrrho
(360 - 270 BC)
People over the world believe in opposite things; knowledge is subjective.
We cannot assume the truth of one explanation rather than any other.
We should just go with the flow - the customs of wherever and whenever we happen to live.

Epicurus
(341 - 270 BC)
All there is in the universe are atoms and space.
Atoms are indestructible and eternal, but their combinations (objects) are always changing.
We, body and mind, are among the ephemeral objects built of atoms.
Don't fear death; so long as you exist, death is not, and when death is, you are not.
Gods are far away and have no desire to be involved with us; it's as if they don't exist.
Happiness in this world should be our aim.
The way to be happy is to withdraw from public life into private communities of like-minded people.
We should enjoy pleasures in moderation, though no non-injurious activity needs to be forbidden.
The greatest pleasure is attainable through knowledge, friendship, and with freedom from fear.

Stoics
(Zeno of Citium, et al.) (334 - 262 BC)
There can be no authority higher than reason.
The world as our reason presents it to us (the world of Nature) is all there is.
Nature is governed by rationally intelligible principles.
The rationality within us and the Nature is what is meant by God.
God is not separate from the world - he is the mind, the self-awareness of the world.
When we die we dissolve back into Nature, there is nowhere else to go.
We should accept our mortality with dignity.
If our emotions rebel against our acceptance of mortality, they are in the wrong.
Emotions are cognitive judgements - forms of knowledge, true and false.
With reason we can eliminate our 'false' emotions; we can then be one with the world.

Timon of Phlius
(320 - 230 BC)
Every proof has premises which it does not itself establish; no ultimate ground of certainty exists.

Plotinus
(204 - 270)
What exists is ultimately mental; for something to be created is for it to be thought.
There are three ascending levels of being: soul (humans), intellect, and good.
We should be in an attempted ascent towards one-ness with the good.

Saint Augustine
(354 - 430)
The flow of time exists for living creature but not for God.
The flow of time does not exist independently of experience.
The present is the inescapable mode of all existence.
Our whole worldly being, including our intellect, is dominated by our will.
To doubt anything, I must exist; I cannot doubt that I exist - I know that with absolute certainty.
There may be other things than our existence that we can know with absolute certainty.
Our soul is non-material and timeless, while our bodies are decaying material objects.
True knowledge is of a realm of timeless and perfect non-material entities.
Our sense can be illusory.
We cannot be saved through our own will; souls who go to Hell are damned by God's choice.

John Scotus Erigena
(815 - 877)
Reason and revelation are both valid ways of arriving at the same truth.
Since God is unknowable, it is impossible for Him to know himself.

Saint Anselm
(1033 - 1109)
Existence is greater than absence; so the most perfect being possible (God) must exist, by definition.

Thomas Aquinas
(1225 - 1274)
Reason and faith are very distinct; we believe in answers that are undecidable by reason.
All our rational knowledge is acquired through sensory experience.
A baby's mind is like a clean slate ('tabula rasa') with nothing written on it.
The 'essence' of a thing is its description, and it's a separate matter from its existence.
The world's essence must have preceded its existence, in God's mind.
God's own essence cannot have preceded his existence; God must be pure existence.
The soul is know by its acts.

William of Ockham
(1287 - 1347)
There is such a thing as necessity in logic but not in the natural order of things.
We cannot learn about the world purely through logical arguemnt; we must first observe.
The more complicated explanation is more likely to have something wrong with it.
Assume the minimum you need in an explanation; entities should not be posited unnecessarily.

Francis Bacon
(1561 - 1626)
The scientific method consists of data collection, hypothesis generation, experiment confirmation, and prediction.
Negative instances are as important as positive ones in guiding us to the right conclusions.
Our senses, feelings and ideologies can be obstacles in our search for reliable knowledge.
Words are but the images of matter, to fall in love with them is to fall in love with a picture.

Thomas Hobbes
(1588 - 1679)
The universe consists of matter in motion, and nothing else exists; it is a vast machine.
Every moving object, including human being, is some sort of machine.
All mental processes consist of movements of matter inside the skull.
Psychological motivations are sorts of pushes: appetites and aversions.
Appetites (liking, love, etc.) are inherently unsatisfiable; they cannot cease until death.
The strongest aversion is the fear of death.
At the bottom, the fear of death causes people to form societies.
In the state of nature there is no order or justice; violence and cunning rules.
The alliances should be such that it is not in anyone's interest to break laws.
Everyone should hand over power to a central authority with absolute power who imposes law.
The central authority holds its power from the people, to protect their freedom and safety.
Any tyranny is better than social chaos.

René Descartes
(1596 - 1650)
In mathematics we start from indubitable premises and deduce irrefutable conclusions.
To gain reliable knowledge, we should start from indubitable premises as in mathematics.
Sensual observations cannot be trusted, as they deceive us frequently.
I cannot be absolutely sure at any moment that I am not being deceived by an evil demon controlling my senses.
The only thing I can be sure of is that I am having the experiences that I am having.
I may not know my own nature or the nature of things, but that I exist is indubitable.
I can think of a perfect being even though I am imperfect; therefore it (God) exists.
If I think carefully, I can be certain of my conclusions, because God will not deceive me.
There are two kinds of substance; mind (which I am as a conscious being) and matter.

Benedict Spinoza
(1632 - 1677)
To understand the world we should apply the methods of mathematics to reality.
Dualism is false because mind, and matter are interacting; they exist in the same realm.
If God is infinite, he cannot have boundaries; everything is part of God, all is one.
Religious and scientific conceptions are two ways of describing the same reality.
Our bodies and our souls are the same being.
Free will is an illusion; we are mostly not aware of the causes of our actions.
Understanding that we are not free can liberate us, coming to terms with things as they are.
Our personal problems are insignificant in the totality of things.
Freedom of speech is necessary to secure the public order.
The true aim of government is liberty.

John Locke
(1632 - 1704)
We should analyze our mental faculties and find out their limits; we cannot know everything.
What we have direct experience of are the contents of our own consciousness.
The data from which we start to think should come through our senses, from the outside reality.
When we are born the mind is like a blank slate on which experience begins to write.
Science can deal with primary (objective, measurable) qualities; length, weight, velocity, etc.
Secondary (subjective) qualities are out of the reach of science: taste, smell, colour, etc.
We can only know the properties of a thing, not the material thing in itself.
I can only know the contents of my awareness, not the thing (self) that is having those experiences.
Possible knowledge consists entirely of transactions between the mysterious subject and object.
Because knowledge/science starts from observation, there is always room for error.
We must always be willing to change our beliefs in the light of changing evidence.
It is mistaken and morally wrong for political and religious authorities to impose beliefs.
As made by God in His own image, mankind was not a jungle beast in the state of nature.
The social contract is made between free men because the presence of government is better.
Sovereignty ultimately remains with the people; they retain their individual rights and freedom.
If the government abuses people's rights, they have the moral right to overthrow it.
If I work to produce something, then I have a right to the fruits of my labour.
I can dispose of my property right as I wish; give it, sell it, etc., independently of government.

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz
(1646 - 1716)
There are two kinds of truths: (analytic) truths of reasoning and (synthetic) truths of fact.
The sufficient reason for a truth of reasoning is to be found by reasoning, without reference to external reality.
The sufficient reason for a truth of fact is to be found by looking at external facts.
There is a huge number of possible worlds.
God, as a perfect being, created the best possible world.
A world with free will is better than one without it; that's why God created this world with evil.
Everything, matter or mind, is made up of centers of activity (monads).
Monads are like dots of consciousness in space.
Human minds are monads.
God is a monad.
Each monad has its own points of view and they do not intersect with each other.
Nature makes no leaps. (The law of continuity)

George Berkeley
(1685 - 1753)
All we can ever apprehend are the contents of our own consciousness.
We know that we exist because we have direct awareness of being a subject of experiences.
We can never be sure that our experiences are attached to objects external to us.
A consistent empiricism leads us to the conclusion that what exists are only minds are their contents.
Total reality exists in God's mind; things exist either in our minds or in God's mind.

David Hume
(1711 - 1776)
It is only from experience that our knowledge of anything outside ourselves can come.
Introspection reveals just a bundle of sensations; the self, the experiencing subject is a fiction.
The existence of God is a question of fact and can be settled only by observation.
There is no observational evidence for the existence of God; only some inferential, indirect arguments.
The Argument from Design is a far cry from proof of a personal God, Christian, or Jew.
Causal connection cannot be observed; correlation is not causation.
However many Xs I observe having the property y, that is no proof that the next X I see will be y.
We should not define a cause-effect relationship as a matter of conceptual necessity.
Theoretically we cannot know anything for sure; practically we have to live as if we do know.
We are usually not driven by reason; reason is the slave of the passions.
Only analytic statements can be known with certainty to be true.
With this level of uncertainty, Theories of Everything (in philosophy, science, etc.) are ridiculous.
We should hold our opinions diffidently, knowing them to be fallible, respecting those of others.
Deductions of 'ought' statements from 'is' statements need careful observation and explanation.
Our moral judgements express our internal, sentimental motivations; they are not derived from reason.
Beauty in things exists in the mind which contemplates them.

Jean-Jacques Rousseau
(1712 - 1778)
Man in a state of nature is a noble savage; human beings are born good, loving justice and order.
Civilization corrupts men, by repressing feels and imposing artificial modes of thinking.
We cannot be uncivilized, so we should change the civilization to make instincts and feelings free.
Education should not discipline a child's natural tendencies; it should encourage them.
The tools of education should not be books but practice: experience of people and things.
Educations should take place in family, not school; and thorough love, not rules and punishment.
Religion is a matter of the heart, not the head. Emotions should dominate instead of reason.
Human society is a collective being with a will of its own.
Laws should be made by everybody deliberating and voting expressing the 'general will'.
The 'general will' might not be good for any individual, but the citizen is subordinate to it.
The rulers can give the task of execution to whoever they choose; a monarch, politicians, etc.
A population at large might constitute an ill-informed, short-sighted legislative body.
'Legislators', charismatic leaders who understand the general will, can draft legislation and persuade people.

Edmund Burke
(1729 - 1797)
Society is so big and complex that a single mind cannot possibly contain and understand it.
Society is like an organism living on its own, not a machine which can be built and modified at will.
The only realistic mode of political change is organic, not revolutionary.
Each generation inherits a treasure from the past which it is its duty to pass on.
Humans are imperfect creatures, therefore the idea of a perfect society is fantasy.
Governments have to deal with people as they are, extremely unequal in talent and ambition.
The public prospers more under practical-minded men than under clever theoreticians.
Great art seeks the infinite, which cannot be clear; it has the emotional pull of the unknown, the sublime.

Immanuel Kant
(1724 - 1804)
What we can know is limited by our sensory organs and brains; these are tools with limitations.
The contents of consciousness, produced by our bodily apparatus, are just limited representations.
Our senses give us accurate information, but they do so as gauges, not pictures.
The mode of existence of things (noumena) has nothing to do with our impressions (phenomena) of them.
We have no means of access to the realm of things as they are (noumena).
It is impossible for any being to understand its own nature.
Space, time, and causality are forms of our sensibility and understanding, not original characteristics of things.
There are synthetic a priori judgements that are based on pure intuitions or our a priori formal representations of space and time.
Existence is not a property; it is a metaphysically necessary condition for the instantiation of any properties.
We cannot know/prove the existence of God/souls because it is out of the limits of our senses.
Science is the key to understand the world of material objects in space and time.
If we are objects moving in space and time with scientific laws, we cannot have free will.
We have moral concepts (good, right, duty, etc.), so we believe that we have free will, at least sometimes.
We believe that there is a non-empirical realm in which decisions are made, that affects our bodies.
Morality is possible only for rational creatures capable of understanding reasons for action.
Because morality concerns the choice of action, moral judgements take the form of imperatives.
Moral world is governed by universal moral laws, just like the empirical world is by scientific laws.
You should act only according to maxims which you can will also to be universal laws.

Jeremy Bentham
(1748 - 1832)
The rightness/wrongness of an actions is determined by its consequences.
Good consequences of actions are those that give pleasure to someone (and bad - pain).
A right course of action is the one that maximizes the excess of pleasure over pain.
Everybody is to count for one; and nobody for more than one.

Mary Wollstonecraft
(1759 - 1797)
Government and all human relations can be simplified, explicated, and rendered transparent.
God made all things right and the cause of all evil is man.
Rights entail duties; none could be expected to perform duties whose natural rights are not respected.
For a truly moral society, a complete change in the nature of the relationship between men and women is required.
Women are ill-prepared for their duties as social beings and imprisoned in a web of false expectations.
Women should be educated like men, to become rational and independent beings whose sense of worth doesn't come from their appearance.
Women should be able to support themselves and their children, and never have to marry or remarry out of financial necessity.
Women should be granted civil and political rights; they should elect representatives of their own.

Johann Gottleib Fichte
(1762 - 1814)
Scientific laws cannot be deduced from empirical observations.
Empirical observations can be deduced from scientific laws.
The universe is a creation of the subject, with logical necessity, from our conception of it.
We have experience of our existence, not as knowing subjects by introspection, but as moral agents.
We know that we persist over time because we know that we bear moral responsibility.
The nature of all reality is moral; it is primarily created by our moral acts.

Friedrich Schelling
(1775 - 1854)
All life is created by Nature out of lifeless matter.
Nature is a process, starting with dead matter, evolving into plants, animals, and then humans.
Everything is one; life is continuous with matter.
Man is part of Nature, not separate from or above it.
Man's creativity is different from the rest of Nature's creativity; it is self-aware.
Since man is part of Nature, in creative arts Nature is becoming self-aware.
The end-point of the process of Nature is self-awareness.
Creative art is the summit of all existence, the raison d'être of reality.
The final desperate question: Why is there anything at all? Why not nothing?

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel
(1770 - 1831)
Geist (spirit-mind) is the stuff of existence.
Everything is the result of an intelligible (not arbitrary) process of change, and everything is changing.
Reality is a historical process where Geist develops towards self-awareness.
Dialectical Process: Thesis (first stage) + Antithesis (reactions, conflicts) = Synthesis (a new thesis)
Change is dominated by historical forces; the individual has no control, he must go with the flow (Zeitgeist)
When Geist will reach self-awareness, everything will be harmonious and change will stop (the Absolute).
In the Absolute, we will be free from conflict, happily serving the whole instead of our own interests.
Our institutions/rules/inventions become external constraints on us, alienating us from ourselves.
We project qualities on God (omniscience, etc.) that alienate us from Him whereas we are one.
One's self-consciousness is dependent on one's recognition of those others as similarly recognizing oneself as a self-conscious subject.

Arthur Schopenhauer
(1788 - 1860)
Without time and space there can be no differentiation.
The noumenal reality, without time and space, cannot consist of things; it is one thing.
Causal connection can exist only within the phenomenal. Noumena cannot cause phenomena.
Will and the bodily movements are the same events known in two different ways (from inside/outside).
The phenomena in the universe requires a vast impersonal, unconscious energy without purpose.
The energy, the force, the impersonal 'will' that we see in the physical events is what noumenal is.
In the phenomenal world, we seem to be separate but ultimately we are one in the noumenal.
The basis of morality is compassion because we are one and we share each other's sufferings.
The concept of a personal God is too anthropomorphic to be taken seriously.
The concept of soul is useless; it supposes mind to be inseparably connected and yet independent of the organism.
The empirical world is without meaning and purpose.
The empirical world seems to be independent of ourselves, but in fact it is all subject-dependent - an illusion.
We should not be taken by the empirical world; we should renounce it.
The end stage of philosophical understanding is the turning away of the human will from the world.
We are slaves to our desires, never fully satisfies; existence is suffering.
The only way in which we can escape the existential suffering is through arts.
Arts give us the experience of being taken out of time and space, out of ourselves.
Music transcends the other arts in metaphysical significance.

Ludwig Feuerbach
(1804 - 1872)
God is an imaginary representation of the human species-essence; theology is anthropology.
When human beings in the course of their history acquire new and different wishes, they worship new and different divinities.
Religion is an alienated form of human self-consciousness: it involves the relation of us to our own essence as though to a being distinct from ourselves.
Theological claims understood n the usual sense (referring to a non-human divine person) are self-contradictory.
To posit matter as God is to affirm atheism and materialism.
We need to renounce the theological essence of religion to affirm its true anthropological essence: the divinity of man.
The hidden 'truth of Christianity' will finally be realized as an atheistic humanism, which embrace the historical tasks of human self-realization.
Thought comes from being, but being does not come from thought.
I am a real, sensuous being and the body in its totality is my ego, my essence itself.

John Stuart Mill
(1806 - 1873)
When we are considering our policies, we should seek the greatest happiness of the greatest number.
The concept of freedom isn't meaningful in the state of nature.
Freedom can flourish only where there are political and legal institutions protecting individual interests and public good.
A person should be free to do anything as long as it doesn't harm anyone else.
There should exist the fullest liberty of professing and discussing any doctrine, however immoral it may be considered.
Over his own body and mind, the individual is sovereign.
The inequality of men-women is a relic from the past; it should be replaced by a system of perfect equality.
The semantic value of a name is its referent.

Charles Darwin
(1809 - 1882)
All the organic beings which have ever lived on this earth have descended from some one primordial form.
There is one general law in the evolution of organisms: multiply, vary, let the best adapted live and the worst adapted die.
It is hardly possible to define clearly what is meant by the organisation of a living thing being higher or lower.
The gradual formation of different languages is similar to that of species; the survival of favored words is natural selection.
The study of nature and man will become far more interesting when we regard every production of nature as one which has had a history.

Søren Kierkegaard
(1813 - 1855)
We have to look at individual entities, not ideas, to understand the world.
The individual human being is the ultimate moral actor; our personal lives, our decisions are important.
We build our lives and ourselves through the choices we make.
The relationship of the individual soul to God is what matters most.
The supreme paradox of all thought is the attempt to discover something that thought cannot think.

Karl Marx
(1818 - 1883)
Reality is a historical dialectical process operating with alienations.
Only when alienations end will we be free and in control, as parts of an organic society instead of selfish individuals.
The world and the dialectical process of change consists of material (not spiritual) forces.
The dialectic works with the development of the means of production and the conflicts between social classes.
Institutions, religions, philosophies, the arts, etc. are determined by the economic substructure.
Religion is man-made; it is the opium of the people.
The Industrialist Capitalist society is the last stage before the end of history, of the dialectic.
The development of technology (belonging to few) is alienating people from the means of production.
The workers will overthrow the capitalists and create a class-free society where the means of production belong to all.

Charles Sanders Peirce
(1839 - 1914)
Knowledge is an activity: a process of doubt/need-to-know, evaluation, and understanding/explaining.
A term whose application makes no difference has no ascertainable meaning - meaning is functional.
Knowledge is an instrument of survival in this world; we acquire it as participants, not as spectators.
Scientific activity isn't adding new certainties to existing ones; it's replacing old explanations with better ones.
Truth is the final opinion on which inquiry is fated to converge.

William James
(1842 - 1910)
Knowledge that does all the jobs required of it (fit the known facts, generate insights/predictions, etc.) is true.
If a statement cannot be disproved and a person benefits from believing in it, he is justified in doing so.

Friedrich Nietzsche
(1844 - 1900)
There is no God and we do not have immortal souls.
We are driven by our wills in this meaningless world full of suffering.
This world is all there is - we should embrace it.
Our current ethics is based on old societies and religions; we should find new foundations for ethics.
Our success as a species was due to the elimination of the weak (stupid, etc.) by the strong (clever, etc.)
Moralists like Jesus created slave-moralities - mediocracies protecting the weak and oppressing the gifted.
The gifted should be able to actualize their 'will to power' in every domain - in politics, in arts, etc.
The will to power is the true happiness for superior people, which is also in the interest of mankind.
We should embrace a new ethics of life-assertion, allowing the superior to become supermen.
We should not fear conflict with others; it makes us develop our abilities.
A life lived to the full doesn't need to derive its meaning from outside itself.
Everything that has happened will happen again in cosmic epicycles of time; this can give motivation for life.

Gottlob Frege
(1848 - 1925)
Logic is independent of human thought; we can learn it, but it's not 'laws of thought.'
Mathematics can be derived from logic.
The 'reference' of an expression is the actual thing corresponding to it - its truth value if it is a proposition.
The 'sense' of an expression s the 'mode of presentation' by which one conceives of the reference, whether or not it exists.
Expressions with different senses can have the same reference.

Edmund Husserl
(1859 - 1938)
The one thing that exists for sure is our consciousness; we should start from there to build our picture of reality.
Our awareness is of objects, but not of ourselves.
Intersubjective (empathic) experience occurs when we consciously attribute intentional acts to other subjects.
In intersubjective experience, I must presuppose that the objects forming my own world exist independently of my subjective perspective.
Intersubjectivity is crucial in our constitution of ourselves as objectively existing subjects, other subjects, and the objective world.

Henri Bergson
(1859 - 1941)
Human beings should be explained from the perspective of evolution.
Our senses do not give us objective representations; they stimulate behavior for survival.
Evolution is directed by a drive ('élan vital') towards greater individuality and complexity.
Everything changes; time is fundamental to reality, to life.
We have direct intuitive experience of time, independent of conceptual thinking and sensual input.
We know we have free will because we have direct intuitive knowledge about our decisions to act.
Our intellect artificially breaks up time and space into manageable units to handle them.
